Sociality and health

Growing evidence from humans and other animals suggests that social relationships can have profound effects on health and lifespan. Individuals with stronger social ties often live longer than those that are socially isolated, yet the mechanisms underlying this relationship remain unclear. How do social interactions influence health and survival? What benefits- and costs- do individuals experience through their social relationships, and how do these ultimately shape fitness outcomes?

During my PhD, working with Prof. Lauren Brent, I studied the long-term rhesus macaque population on Cayo Santiago to address these questions. I examined how both affiliative and competitive social interactions influence markers of health and survival, including immune cell counts, parasite load and injury-related mortality. This work combined long-term behavioural data, social network analyses, field observations, and physiological sampling, and was part of a large-scale collaboration between universities across Europe and North America.

Key publications:#

Pavez-Fox MA et al. (2022): We found that macaques with more affiliative partners (particularly close relatives) and higher social status were less likely to be injured and therefore less likely to die from injuries. However, social relationships did not improve survival after an injury had occurred. This study identified a potential mechanim through which social relationships can influence health and lifespan.

Pavez-Fox MA et al. (2024): We examined how affiliative relationships and social status related to parasite infection risk. Individuals with more social partners and higher social status were less likely to be infected with Balantidium coli. However, social behaviour and parasite prevalence also varied following a major hurricane, suggesting that the health effects of social relatioships can depend on ecological conditions.
